IPA & Independent Mortgage Broker in Singapore: Why Both Matter Before You Sign an OTP

Two of the most expensive mistakes a Singapore property buyer can make are signing an OTP without an In-Principle Approval, and assuming any mortgage rate they are quoted is the rate they qualify for. The fix to both costs nothing and takes 48 hours: secure a real IPA, and engage an independent broker who acts on your behalf, not the bank’s.

Why an IPA is Non-Negotiable Before OTP

An Option to Purchase commits real money. For private property, that is 1% of the purchase price as option fee — S$15,000 on a S$1.5M condo. For HDB resale, the option fee can run up to S$1,000 plus a 5% deposit on exercise. If you cannot complete the bank loan within the 14-day option window, the deposit is forfeited. The seller keeps it. There is no negotiation.

An In-Principle Approval (IPA) is the bank’s written commitment that, subject to property valuation, they will lend you a specific quantum at a specific tenure. It validates your income, debt servicing ratios (TDSR for private property at 55%, MSR for HDB at 30%), and your MAS 4% stress test capacity, all before you sign anything binding. Without an IPA, you are betting the option fee on assumptions.

IPA vs Eligibility Check: Not the Same Thing

Bank websites offer free “eligibility calculators”. These are not IPAs. They are unverified self-assessments — you key in income, the calculator returns a TDSR estimate. The bank has not seen a payslip, a CPF contribution history, an IRAS Notice of Assessment, or your credit bureau report.

A real IPA requires document submission: latest 3 months of payslips, latest CPF contribution history, latest 12 months of bank statements, NRIC, and (for self-employed applicants) two years of NOAs. The bank’s underwriter signs off in writing. That document is what protects your option fee — not a number on a webpage.

The 14-Day OTP Clock

The standard private property OTP is 14 calendar days, weekends and public holidays included. Inside those 14 days the buyer must:

  • Confirm the bank’s formal Letter of Offer at the IPA quantum
  • Order an independent property valuation (typically 2–3 working days)
  • Engage a conveyancing solicitor and run preliminary title checks
  • Coordinate CPF Board for OA usage and accrued interest projections
  • Sign the Letter of Offer and exercise the OTP within the window

That is a tight calendar. Starting the IPA process after signing OTP is the single most common scramble we see. The remedy is simple: secure the IPA first, then go shopping with confidence on what quantum the property must fall under.

Independent Broker vs Walking into Your Bank

The most common path for a Singapore buyer is calling the relationship manager at their existing bank. That call gets you exactly one set of packages — that bank’s. Singapore has 16+ MAS-regulated lenders offering mortgages (see the Association of Banks in Singapore member list). The other 15 are out of view.

The economics matter: an independent broker is paid by the bank on disbursement, regardless of which bank the borrower picks. The broker’s incentive is the borrower closing any qualifying loan. The bank officer’s incentive is closing their loan, even if a competitor offers 0.20% sharper.

The Bias Problem — and How Brokers Solve It

Bank officers are not malicious. They are quota-bound. The package they highlight is the package their KPI rewards. That is structural, not personal. The buyer who only speaks to one bank is, by definition, getting a biased recommendation — a single bank cannot represent the market.

“Every borrower who walks into a single bank gets a tailored answer to the wrong question. The right question is not what does this bank offer? — it is what does the entire market offer at my exact loan size?”

An independent broker does not have a quota with any single lender. Nexus, for example, runs simultaneous IPAs across multiple banks on the same set of borrower documents, then lays the offers side by side: headline rate, lock-in, prepayment terms, legal subsidy, cashback, and what the rate reverts to after the fixed window. The borrower sees the spread, picks the package, and signs only with the chosen bank.

Loan Size Changes the Rate: Tiered Pricing

Singapore banks tier mortgage pricing by loan quantum. The same applicant with the same income, applying on the same day, will be quoted different rates depending on whether the loan is S$400K, S$900K, or S$1.6M. The spread between tiers is typically 0.10%–0.25% p.a.

This is the part most buyers do not realise: the same bank publishes different rate cards for different loan quantums. A buyer borrowing S$450K and a buyer borrowing S$1.5M on the same package, on the same day, walk away with different effective rates. It is not a negotiation tactic — it is the bank’s pricing engine.

Why this matters in dollars

On a S$1.2M loan over 25 years, the difference between a 1.55% rate and a 1.40% rate is roughly S$95 per month, or about S$28,500 over 25 years. That is the cost of being placed in the wrong tier — or of not knowing the next tier existed. A broker that pulls all 16 lenders sees which bank prices most aggressively at the borrower’s exact quantum. A single bank officer cannot.

Mortgage Isn’t One Size Fits All

Rate is one variable. The package wraps a dozen others, and the right combination depends on the buyer’s plans for the property:

  • Lock-in length. 2-year vs 3-year fixed. A buyer planning to decouple in 18 months should not be in a 3-year lock-in.
  • Partial prepayment terms. Some packages allow 50% prepayment with no penalty after year 1; others lock you in fully. Material if a bonus or business sale is on the horizon.
  • Sale clause. Most lock-in penalties (1.5% of outstanding) are waived for genuine sales. Confirm this is in the Letter of Offer if the property may be sold within lock-in.
  • Fixed vs SORA-linked. Fixed gives certainty; SORA tracks the rate cycle. The right pick depends on rate-view, holding period, and stress tolerance.
  • Legal subsidy and cashback. A S$2,500 legal subsidy with a 3-year clawback is not the same as S$2,500 cashback with a 2-year clawback. Different exit math.
  • Reversion rate. What the rate becomes after the fixed window matters more than the headline. Some packages step to 3.50%–4.50% board rates if not actively repriced.
  • Bank’s underwriting flexibility. Some banks weight rental income harder, some accept overseas income, some are stricter on self-employed NOAs.

None of these are visible from a published rate card. They surface in the Letter of Offer fine print — which a broker reads regularly across all 16 lenders.

Planning the Exit and the Next Purchase

A first mortgage signed in isolation tends to handicap the second purchase. A first mortgage planned alongside the second — lock-in alignment, TDSR headroom, decoupling feasibility, ABSD remission timing — preserves the next move.

Most Singapore households make 2 to 4 property transactions across their working life. The first mortgage either preserves the optionality of the next move, or quietly closes it off. A few examples of how the first loan shapes the second:

  • Decoupling readiness. If you may transfer ownership to one spouse to free the other for a second purchase (saving 20% ABSD on a S$1.5M condo — S$300,000), the first mortgage’s lock-in clock and seller’s stamp duty (SSD) timeline must allow it. Decoupling math needs runway.
  • ABSD remission window. Singapore Citizens upgrading from one home to another can apply for ABSD remission if the first property is sold within 6 months of the new completion. The first loan’s lock-in must align, or the 1.5% redemption penalty stacks onto the upgrade cost.
  • TDSR headroom for property #2. Every existing mortgage instalment counts against TDSR on the next loan. A higher first-loan tenure reduces monthly outflow and preserves TDSR room. Cheap rate, high instalment is sometimes the wrong trade.
  • LTV stair-step. First property loan: up to 75% LTV. Second loan with first still outstanding: up to 45% LTV. The cash-and-CPF gap (S$300K–S$500K extra on a S$1.5M purchase) needs planning, often via a strategic equity / cash-out loan on the first.
  • Pledge-fund continuity. A pledge-fund used to lift TDSR on loan #1 may be needed again at loan #2. Sequencing matters.

A bank officer optimises today’s file. A broker thinking two transactions ahead will sometimes recommend a slightly higher rate today if it preserves a much larger benefit at purchase #2. That is the dimension a single bank rarely surfaces.

How a Nexus Broker Actually Works

Nexus Mortgage SG is paid by the bank on disbursement. The service is free to the borrower. The mechanic for a typical engagement:

  • Day 1. 30-minute call. We map your purchase plan, future moves, current debts, income mix, CPF position. No documents needed yet.
  • Day 2. Document checklist sent. You upload payslips, CPF contribution history, NRIC, latest bank statements, NOAs (if applicable). Roughly 15 minutes of admin.

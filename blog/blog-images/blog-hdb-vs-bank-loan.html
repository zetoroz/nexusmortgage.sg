HDB vs Bank Loans: Which Is Right for You in 2026?

A definitive comparison of HDB concessionary loans and bank loans — eligibility, interest rates, flexibility, and the true cost of each over a 25-year tenure.

The Decision That Shapes the Next 25 Years

For most Singaporeans purchasing an HDB flat, there is a fork in the road that receives far less attention than it deserves: do you take the HDB concessionary loan, or do you finance through a bank? Both routes lead to home ownership, but the journey — and the total cost — can differ by tens of thousands of dollars over the life of a loan.

The decision is not merely about securing the lowest rate today. It involves eligibility rules, down payment structures, the ability to use your CPF savings, refinancing flexibility, and how well each option serves your long-term financial plan. What looks like a straightforward choice on the surface contains several layers of nuance that many buyers discover only after the fact.

This article gives you a clear, numbers-grounded comparison of both options so you can walk into your purchase with complete clarity.

The HDB Concessionary Loan

The HDB concessionary loan is a government-backed financing facility offered exclusively through the Housing & Development Board. It is available only for HDB flat purchases — not executive condominiums, not private property — and it comes with a specific set of eligibility requirements that buyers must satisfy.

Eligibility Criteria

To qualify for an HDB loan, all buyers and occupiers listed on the application must meet the following conditions as of 2026:

  • At least one buyer must be a Singapore Citizen (Singapore Permanent Residents buying with another SPR may apply, subject to conditions)
  • Monthly household income must not exceed S$14,000 (or S$21,000 for extended family applications, and S$7,000 for singles)
  • No private residential property must have been disposed of within 30 months before the application
  • No concurrent ownership of or interest in any local or overseas private residential property
  • All buyers must not have previously taken two or more HDB housing loans

The income ceiling is a hard limit. If your household income exceeds S$14,000, you are automatically ineligible for an HDB loan and must finance through a bank. For many dual-income households in Singapore’s professional sector, this is increasingly a binding constraint rather than a theoretical one.

The Interest Rate Structure

The HDB loan rate is not independently set by HDB. It is pegged at the CPF Ordinary Account interest rate plus 0.1%. With the CPF OA currently paying 2.5% per annum, the HDB concessionary loan rate stands at 2.6% per annum for 2026.

This rate can only change if MAS adjusts the CPF OA rate — which has remained at 2.5% since January 2008, providing borrowers with 17 years of rate stability. That historical consistency is a meaningful form of protection against the rate volatility that bank loans inherently carry.

“The HDB rate has not changed in nearly two decades. That stability has a value that never shows up in a simple rate comparison spreadsheet.”

Loan-to-Value and Down Payment

An HDB loan permits borrowing up to 80% of the flat’s purchase price or value, whichever is lower. The remaining 20% can be paid entirely using CPF Ordinary Account savings — no cash is required for the down payment itself, which is a meaningful advantage for buyers who have been diligently contributing to their CPF but have limited liquidity.

The Bank Loan

A bank loan for an HDB flat is sourced from a licensed financial institution — DBS, OCBC, UOB, Maybank, Standard Chartered, HSBC, and over a dozen others operating in Singapore. Unlike the HDB loan, there is no income ceiling for eligibility. Instead, your borrowing capacity is governed by the MAS TDSR and MSR framework, which constrains what the bank can lend to you based on your debt ratios.

Rate Types: Fixed, Floating, and Board Rate

Bank loans offer flexibility in rate structure that the HDB loan does not. In 2026, the most popular packages available to HDB buyers are:

  • Fixed-rate packages: Rates locked for 2–5 years, typically between 1.40% and 1.80% p.a. in Q2 2026, reverting to a floating rate thereafter
  • SORA-pegged packages: Rate moves with the Singapore Overnight Rate Average, currently in the 1.5%–1.8% range for three-month compounded SORA plus bank spread
  • Board rate packages: Rate set at the bank’s discretion; less common for new applications but still found in some packages

The competition across 16+ banks means the best rate for your profile is rarely found by calling just one lender. Rates vary by loan quantum, income profile, and property type, and promotional packages rotate frequently throughout the year.

Loan-to-Value and Down Payment

Bank loans on HDB flats are subject to a maximum LTV of 75% of the purchase price or valuation. The down payment is therefore a minimum of 25%, of which at least 5% must be paid in cash. The remaining 20% may come from CPF OA savings or additional cash.

This cash requirement is the most material practical difference for first-time buyers. An HDB flat purchased at S$600,000 requires a minimum S$30,000 in cash if financing with a bank, versus zero cash down with an HDB loan.

The True Cost Over 25 Years

Rate comparisons only tell part of the story. What a buyer needs to understand is the total interest cost over the life of the loan — because a lower starting rate is not automatically a lower long-term cost if that rate resets to a higher floating rate after the initial fixed period.

Consider a buyer purchasing an HDB flat at S$600,000, with a down payment of S$150,000 (25%), leaving a loan amount of S$450,000 over a 25-year tenure. We compare two scenarios:

The striking takeaway is that despite offering a lower initial monthly payment, the bank loan in this scenario costs more in total interest over 25 years — because the post-lock-in floating rate more than offsets the early-year savings. The HDB loan’s higher starting rate is predictable and stays constant; the bank loan’s true cost depends heavily on where rates sit after the fixed period ends.

Buyers who refinance their bank loan regularly — every two to three years — can manage this risk effectively. But refinancing requires effort, involves legal and valuation fees (typically S$1,500–S$3,000), and requires active monitoring. The HDB loan demands none of that.

“The bank loan’s initial rate advantage can evaporate in years four and five. The buyers who win are those who refinance proactively — not those who set and forget.”

When the HDB Loan Is the Right Choice

  • You have limited cash savings. The HDB loan’s zero cash down payment requirement is a genuine advantage. If your CPF is well-funded but your bank balance is modest, the HDB loan removes a significant barrier to entry.
  • You value rate certainty above all else. The HDB rate has not changed in nearly two decades. For buyers who sleep better knowing their monthly obligation cannot shift unexpectedly, this predictability has real psychological and practical value.
  • You do not want to monitor and refinance. Bank loan optimisation requires attention. If you prefer a set-and-forget financing structure, the HDB loan delivers exactly that.
  • You are close to the income ceiling. If your household income is well within the S$14,000 threshold, retaining the option to take an HDB loan keeps a valuable fallback in place. Once you forfeit eligibility — by owning private property, for example — it is gone permanently.
  • You may want flexibility to exit early. The HDB loan carries no lock-in penalty. You can make lump-sum repayments or refinance to a bank whenever market conditions favour it.

When the Bank Loan Is the Right Choice

  • You exceed the S$14,000 income ceiling. You have no choice but to use a bank loan. The focus then shifts to securing the best package across all available lenders.
  • You have sufficient cash for the 5% down payment requirement. If cash liquidity is not a constraint, the bank loan’s lower initial rate translates into meaningful short-term savings.
  • You plan to sell or refinance within 5–7 years. If you are likely to exit the property within the fixed-rate window — upgrading, downsizing, or relocating — the bank loan’s initial rate advantage is fully captured without exposure to the post-lock-in floating period.
  • You are buying an executive condominium. HDB loans are not available for ECs. A bank loan is the only option, and shopping across lenders becomes essential.
  • You are buying a resale flat at a significant valuation gap. For high-quantum resale transactions where the purchase price materially exceeds HDB’s assessed valuation, the difference in LTV (80% HDB vs 75% bank) matters less — and the bank’s broader flexibility in quantum may be more relevant.

The Factor Everyone Overlooks: The One-Way Door

There is an asymmetry in the HDB–bank decision that few buyers appreciate at the point of purchase: you can always move from an HDB loan to a bank loan, but you cannot move back.

Once you refinance an HDB flat from an HDB loan to a bank loan, HDB will not re-admit you to the concessionary loan scheme for that property. The option is extinguished permanently. This has two important implications.

First, taking an HDB loan initially and refinancing to a bank loan later — once you have built equity and rates have moved in your favour — is a perfectly rational strategy. You capture the HDB loan’s zero-cash down payment and rate stability in the early years, then migrate to a competitive bank package when your financial position and market conditions align.

Second, the decision to take a bank loan at the outset is irreversible. If you later wish rates were lower or circumstances change, you cannot restore the concessionary rate. For buyers on the margin — those who qualify for the HDB loan but are tempted by a lower bank rate today — this optionality has genuine value.

A Note on the MAS Stress Test

If you are financing through a bank, both the TDSR and MSR — assessed at the MAS 4% stress-test floor rate — determine how much you can borrow. Even if your bank offers a rate of 1.65%, the bank is required to assess your affordability as if you were paying 4.0%. This significantly reduces many buyers’ assessed borrowing capacity relative to what the actual monthly payment would be.

For HDB flats, the MSR applies a second, tighter constraint: your stress-tested monthly repayment cannot exceed 30% of gross monthly income. At 4%, a gross monthly income of S$8,000 limits the HDB bank loan quantum to approximately S$503,000 over 25 years. This is frequently the binding constraint rather than the TDSR.

HDB’s own loan assessment does not use the 4% floor. HDB assesses your eligibility using its own internal criteria, which generally result in a more generous qualifying quantum than bank stress-testing — another practical advantage of the concessionary loan route for buyers who are near the bank’s MSR ceiling.

Making the Decision: Where to Start

There is no universally correct answer between HDB and bank loans. The right choice depends on your income relative to the S$14,000 ceiling, your current cash liquidity, your appetite for rate monitoring, your expected holding period, and your longer-term property plans. What is clear is that the decision deserves careful analysis — not a default to whichever option a single adviser or a single bank happens to present.

The most common mistake is treating the two options as interchangeable beyond the headline rate. The LTV difference, the cash down payment requirement, the one-way refinancing door, and the total interest cost over the full loan tenure are all meaningful factors that compound over 25 years in ways that are not immediately obvious from a rate table alone.

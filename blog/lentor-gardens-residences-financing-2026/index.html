Lentor Gardens Residences (2026): The Cheapest Land in Lentor — and How to Finance the Launch

By Dan Ler, Mortgage Advisor

Short answer

Lentor Gardens Residences sits on the cheapest land in the Lentor cycle. Developer Kingsford secured the District 26 site at about S$920 psf ppr — below every other Lentor parcel (Modern S$1,204, Hills S$1,060, Mansion S$985, Central Residences S$982) and far under the record S$1,278 psf ppr paid for the March 2026 Lentor Central parcel. That land-cost edge points to a keen indicative price around S$2,150 psf for the ~500-unit, 99-year leasehold project. The financing side is standard private-property: 75% LTV, TDSR 55% at the MAS 4% stress floor, BUC progressive payments over ~3–4 years, and an IPA-before-OTP to protect your 1% option fee.

The Headline: Cheapest Land in the Lentor Cycle

The Lentor precinct has been Singapore's most-watched cluster of new launches since 2021 — seven sites, one estate. What sets Lentor Gardens Residences apart is the number underneath it: Government Land Sales records put Kingsford Development's winning bid at about S$429.23m, or roughly S$920 per square foot per plot ratio (psf ppr) — the lowest land cost of any site in the precinct.

The contrast is the story. The very next Lentor parcel, awarded in March 2026, set a record S$1,278 psf ppr — roughly 39% more expensive land than Lentor Gardens. A developer's land cost is the single largest input into its eventual selling price, so a project that bought land ~S$360 psf ppr cheaper than the latest tender starts the launch with a structural pricing advantage.

Why a Low Land Cost Matters to a Buyer

Land cost is not an abstract developer metric — it flows directly into the price you pay and the margin of safety you buy with. Three practical implications for a Lentor Gardens buyer:

  • A lower launch psf. With cheaper land, the developer can hit its target margin at a lower selling price. Market commentary places Lentor Gardens at or below its newer neighbours rather than above them — unusual for the latest launch in a maturing estate.
  • A built-in value reference. When the next Lentor launch is tipped to start near S$2,700 psf off that record S$1,278 land, an earlier buyer at ~S$2,150 psf is holding the cheaper basis in the same micro-market — the kind of intra-precinct gap that supports resale and exit liquidity.
  • Mature, established surroundings. Lentor is no longer a frontier. Lentor Modern's mall, the MRT (Thomson–East Coast Line), and several completed or near-complete neighbours mean the amenity base is already in, not promised.

None of this is investment advice — resale outcomes depend on the wider market, your holding period, and cooling measures such as Seller's Stamp Duty and ABSD. But the land-cost gap is a real, quantifiable edge, and it is the right lens through which to size your financing.

Financing a Lentor Gardens Unit — the 75% LTV Math

Lentor Gardens sits firmly in the private-property bank-loan universe: no HDB concessionary financing, no MSR cap, full MAS Notice 645 TDSR at 55% of gross monthly income. Maximum loan-to-value is 75% on a first property, with a tenure capped at 30 years or age 65 (whichever is shorter).

Upfront cash and stamp duty

On an illustrative S$2.04M (~3-bedroom) unit:

  • 5% cash booking fee (~S$102,000) — on Option to Purchase exercise.
  • 20% cash or CPF (~S$408,000) — on Sale & Purchase signing.
  • Buyer's Stamp Duty (~S$71,600 on S$2.04M) — within 14 days of OTP. IRAS BSD schedule.
  • ABSD if applicable — 20% for Singapore Citizens on a second residential property, higher for PRs and foreigners. IRAS ABSD schedule.
  • 75% bank loan (~S$1.53M) — disbursed progressively through construction.

CPF and the long-tail cost

CPF Ordinary Account funds can cover the cash-or-CPF portion and ongoing instalments, within the standard limits at the CPF Home Ownership portal. Remember each CPF dollar used accrues at 2.5% p.a., compounded monthly, until you sell — so plan the OA drawdown deliberately rather than depleting it on day one.

Progressive Payments Through Construction

As a Building-Under-Construction (BUC) project, Lentor Gardens uses the Normal Payment Scheme: the bank disburses your loan in stages as the developer hits construction milestones, not as a lump sum. The schedule is statutory:

Monthly instalments rise stage by stage as more of the loan disburses. Early on, your outlay is modest — the bank charges interest only on the drawn 10–20%. By TOP you are servicing the full loan. Model the TOP-month instalment now, because that is the cashflow you must carry for the next 25–30 years. TDSR & the 4% Stress Test by Quantum

MAS forces banks to assess every Singapore home loan against a 4% medium-term interest-rate floor, regardless of the rate you actually lock. Your eligibility is sized against that 4% — not today's ~1.35%–1.40% fixed — and the 55% TDSR ceiling is applied to that stressed instalment.

Indicative monthly stress-test instalments at 4% over 30 years, by loan quantum:

Tenure assumption. The 30-year column assumes the borrower is age 35 or younger at purchase — the longest tenure that still keeps 75% LTV requires loan maturity by age 65. Buyers in their 40s must shorten tenure to fit the age-65 rule (raising the instalment) or accept 55% LTV with 45% cash/CPF down.

Income floors are gross-monthly equivalents before existing debt — car loans, credit-card minimums, personal loans — is deducted from the 55% headroom. Variable bonus and self-employed income take haircuts; see our self-employed TDSR guide. IPA Timing — Book the Loan, Not Just the Unit

Singapore's Option-to-Purchase timeline is unforgiving. You pay a 1% Option Fee on booking, get 21 days to decide, and forfeit it if you walk. An In-Principle Approval sized against your actual income documents protects that money — skip it and the OTP deposit is at risk if a bank later declines.

An independent broker can return an IPA in about 14 days by running your file across multiple MAS-regulated banks in parallel, not sequentially. On a Lentor launch where the keenest-priced stacks tend to clear first, the IPA is your entry ticket to the allocation you actually want — not the leftover stack.

Fixed vs SORA for a BUC Purchase

As of mid-2026, our live rate comparison shows fixed packages starting around 1.35%–1.40% p.a. on qualifying loan sizes, and SORA-pegged floating tracking 1M/3M Compounded SORA plus a bank spread. Quote on the day you lock — SORA moves daily.

For a BUC purchase with a 3–4 year construction tail, the rate question splits in two:

  1. Construction phase (years 0–3). Only a fraction of the loan is drawn at any moment, so a rate move on a 20–30% drawn loan is far smaller in dollar terms than on a fully disbursed one.
  2. Post-TOP phase (year 4 onward). Full loan, full instalment — this is where the package actually bites, for 25+ years.

A common 2026 pattern: a 2-year fixed during early drawdowns for cashflow certainty, then a re-quote at TOP when the loan is fully disbursed and the market offers a wider menu. The right call depends on your TDSR headroom, second-property plans and break-cost tolerance.

"On a new launch, the cheaper land is the developer's margin of safety. Your margin of safety is the loan you can still carry at the 4% stress floor on the day the whole loan is drawn — not the teaser instalment in month one."

Three Moves Before You OTP

  1. Run the TDSR check at the tier you actually want. A ~2BR and a ~4BR sit roughly S$7,000/mo apart in stress-test income floor — the right unit is the one your TDSR/MSR affordability check clears at the 4% floor, not the one a bonus year can pretend to carry.
  2. Get an IPA in ~14 days. An independent IPA run across multiple banks in parallel protects your 1% OTP fee and keeps you in the allocation you want.
  3. Plan the TOP-month cashflow, not the booking-month one. Build the progressive-payment schedule against the 4% floor and confirm the full-disbursement instalment survives a downside scenario.
